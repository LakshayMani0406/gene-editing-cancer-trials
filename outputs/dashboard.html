Gene-Editing & Cell Therapy
Cancer Trials Analytics

This project is an end-to-end data science pipeline built on 4,460 real clinical trials from ClinicalTrials.gov spanning 1990–2025. It analyzes how scientists are using gene-editing tools — CRISPR, CAR-T cells, NK cells, oncolytic viruses — to fight cancer. The goal is to understand which approaches are working, which cancers are being neglected, and what predicts a trial's success.

🔬 The Science — What Is Gene Editing?
✂️

CRISPR-Cas9

Molecular scissors that cut DNA at precise locations. Scientists use it to knock out cancer-protecting genes (like PD-1 in T cells) or insert new instructions. First human trials began in 2019.

Gene: PDCD1, TRAC, B2M
🎯

CAR-T Cell Therapy

A patient's T cells are extracted, engineered in a lab to recognize cancer's "name tag" (like CD19), then infused back. They seek and destroy cancer cells like GPS-guided missiles.

Target: CD19, BCMA, CD33
🔑

TCR Therapy

Similar to CAR-T but uses T-cell receptors instead of artificial receptors. Can target proteins inside cancer cells — invisible to CAR-T — by recognizing peptide fragments on the cell surface.

Target: NY-ESO, MAGE, WT1
🌿

TIL Therapy

Tumor-infiltrating lymphocytes — the immune cells already fighting cancer inside the tumor — are extracted, expanded 1,000× in a lab, and re-infused. Proven effective in melanoma.

First success: Melanoma (1988)
⚡

NK Cell Therapy

Natural Killer cells don't need to recognize a specific target to kill — they attack anything that looks "wrong." Unlike CAR-T, they can be used off-the-shelf from donors without immune rejection.

No MHC matching needed
💉

mRNA & Gene Therapy

mRNA instructs cells to make tumor antigens for vaccines (same platform as COVID-19 shots). Gene therapy uses viral vectors (AAV, lentivirus) to deliver corrective or therapeutic genes directly.

Platform: lipid nanoparticle

Statistics

What the Statistics Tell Us

We ran 9 formal statistical tests to find what actually predicts a trial succeeding. 5 came back with definitive answers. The biggest: larger trials complete more often (the more patients, the better). Trial phase matters too — Phase III trials don't finish as often as Phase I. Surprisingly, whether it's a blood cancer or solid tumor makes no difference in success rate.

Can a Computer Predict Trial Success?

We trained 11 machine learning models to predict whether a cancer trial will succeed or fail, using features like cancer type, how many patients enrolled, trial phase, and who's funding it. The best model (Soft Voting Ensemble) correctly predicts outcomes 90% of the time — far better than random guessing. This confirms that trial success is not random: it follows patterns.

The Research Explosion in Plain English

In 2013, only 3 scientific papers existed on using CRISPR to fight cancer. By 2024, there were 1,288 — a 43,000% explosion. CAR-T research also hit 1,022 papers in 2024. Publication volume is a leading indicator: when scientists write more papers about a therapy, clinical trials follow 2-3 years later. Watch AI+CRISPR — it's the fastest-growing research area right now.
