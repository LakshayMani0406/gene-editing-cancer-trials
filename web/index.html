The two facts that broke the "clean" number

of all trials labelled a "success" had never actually completed. They were still recruiting or active, counted as wins only because they had not failed yet. Those trials are recent, so the model could guess the label from the calendar.

AUC

comes from the trial's start year alone. That is of the "registration-only" model's signal above chance. The honest-looking 0.72 was mostly a recency detector.

Why I report all three numbers

The 0.90 is what the data hands you if you are not careful: it includes how many patients enrolled and whether results were posted, both of which are only known after a trial runs. A terminated trial mechanically has low enrollment and no posted results, so the model was reading the outcome off its own inputs.

The 0.72 is what you get after that obvious fix. It still fooled me. The success label counted recent, still-running trials as wins, so the model scored points just for noticing a trial was new.

The ~0.60 is the number I can stand behind: real completions versus real terminations, trained on the past to predict the future. It sits just above a coin flip. Honest prediction of trial completion from registration data is close to impossible, and that is the result. I would rather show that and explain it than ship the 0.90 and be wrong in production.
